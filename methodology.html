The Methodology

We score every burger across 20 criteria, each on a four-point scale, for a maximum of 80 points. No secret weighting. No vibes-only verdicts. Here is exactly how the sausage — and the patty — gets made.

Step One

The Four-Point Scale

Every criterion gets a whole number from 1 to 4. We keep it tight on purpose — a narrow scale forces a decision and stops everyone defaulting to a polite "7 out of 10."

Step Two

The Twenty Criteria

Grouped into six families. Each family rolls up into the radar you see on every scorecard.

Step Three

The Maths

Totals are computed, never trusted

A burger's total is the sum of its 20 sub-scores, full stop. If an old paper card says "70" but the columns add up to 68, the ledger records 68. The sum is the truth — it keeps everyone honest and makes every score reproducible.

Worked example — the first entry

Shake Shack's double ShackBurger scored 4·3·4·3·4 / 4·3·3 / 3·4·3·3 / 3·3·4·3 / 3·4 / 3·4 across the six families. That sums to 68 / 80 — which is the number on the ledger, regardless of what was scribbled in the "Total" box on the night.

When more than one of us scores the same burger

Each criterion is averaged across everyone who rated that exact burger at that restaurant. The radar plots those per-family averages; the headline number is the average of everyone's totals. One person's hot take can't run the table.

How the rankings are ordered

The leaderboard sorts by mean total out of 80. When two burgers tie, we break it in this order:

  1. Higher average on The Patty — the part you cannot fake.
  2. Higher average on Craveability — would you go back tomorrow?
  3. More club ratings on record — consensus beats a single voice.
  4. The most recent rating wins, because momentum counts.

The two honest questions

Beyond the 80 points, every card answers two yes/no questions that the score alone can miss:

  • Order again? — Would you personally order this exact burger next time?
  • Club recommends? — Would you send a friend across town for it?

Governance

The rubric lives in one file — data/rubric.json — and this page is generated from it, so the methodology can never quietly drift from what we actually score. Changing a criterion is a visible edit on the public record. Every rating arrives as its own entry in data/ratings.json; nothing is added in the dark.
